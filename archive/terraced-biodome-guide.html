Design Overview

The Terraced BioDome is a 24m¬≤ half-dome that uses an existing stone terrace wall as its back wall. It's the most affordable design and ideal for sloped sites with sturdy terrace walls.

Prerequisite: Suitable Terrace Wall

This design requires an existing stone terrace wall that meets structural requirements. Complete the Wall Assessment section before proceeding.

Terrace Wall Assessment

Critical evaluation before committing to this design

Before committing materials and labor, conduct a thorough structural evaluation of your existing terrace wall. This is the most important step for the Terraced BioDome.

Minimum Requirements

  • Structural integrity: No large displacement, bulging, or collapses
  • Height: Minimum 2.8m (roof ridge rests at wall top)
  • Length: At least 10m of usable wall
  • Face condition: Reasonably flat (not severely convex)
  • Base stability: No signs of tipping, settling, or undercutting
  • Drainage: Water moves away from wall

Visual Inspection Categories

Category 1: Overall Geometry
  • Walk full length at 2m intervals, take photos
  • Look for bulging: Measure deviation from plumb. Any bulge >5cm is a concern
  • Check for rotation: Does top lean outward? Indicates slip plane
  • Measure height at minimum 3 points
  • Record visible cracks: Note orientation, length, width
Category 2: Joint Condition
  • Examine mortar joints: Probe with screwdriver‚Äîshould require firm pressure
  • Check for open joints: Gaps >2cm = concern
  • Look for plant roots in joints
  • Tap with hammer: Solid sound = good; hollow = poor quality
Category 3: Drainage Evidence
  • White mineral staining on wall face = water movement
  • Look for wet zones at base (poor drainage behind wall)
  • Identify weep holes (good) or blocked drains (bad)
  • Check for erosion channels on face
Category 4: Base Stability
  • Examine footing visibility: Can you see the base?
  • Check for undercutting: Water erosion undermining base?
  • Look for settlement: Different heights at wall ends?
  • Measure distance from wall toe to erosion

Foundation: Semicircular Gabion Arc

The foundation is a 180¬∞ arc of gabion baskets that ties into the terrace wall at both ends, creating a complete structural ring.

Specifications

  • Shape: Semicircular arc (180¬∞), radius ~4.5m
  • Width: 60cm (ring width from inner to outer face)
  • Height: 50cm above grade, 30cm below (total 80cm)
  • Connections: Both ends tie into terrace wall base using steel plates or through-stones

Construction Sequence

1

Mark the Arc

Using a stake at center point (against terrace wall), mark a 4.5m radius semicircle with string. Both ends should touch the terrace wall base.

2

Excavate Arc Trench

Dig 30cm deep √ó 70cm wide along the semicircular arc. Level bottom carefully.

3

Tie into Terrace Wall

At both arc ends, install steel plates embedded into gabion that connect to terrace wall. May require drilling into wall base or using through-stones.

4

Fill with Stone

Assemble gabion mesh cages, fill with sorted granite. Level top course with flat bearing stones for post seating.

Wall Framework

Vertical Posts

  • Curved front posts: ~14 posts on semicircular front
  • Wall-end posts: 2 additional posts where arc meets terrace wall
  • Diameter: 15-20cm
  • Spacing: ~1.2m apart around the arc

Horizontal Wattle

  • Quantity: ~98 poles (~7 levels √ó 14 spans)
  • Diameter: 5-8cm
  • Wall thickness: 35-45cm light straw-clay (same as Standard)

Weave horizontals between vertical posts, alternating inside/outside for each level.

Lean-to Roof Structure

Unlike the Standard BioDome's reciprocal roof, the Terraced design uses a simple lean-to structure with parallel rafters spanning from the curved front wall to the terrace wall.

Roof Specifications

  • Rafters: 14-15 poles, 15-18cm diameter, 4m length
  • Spacing: ~1.2m apart (matching wall post spacing)
  • Base (eaves): 3.0m height at curved front wall
  • Ridge: 5.0m height at terrace wall
  • Slope: 25-30¬∞ pitch
  • Overhang: 1.0-1.5m beyond curved wall for weather protection

Ledger Board

The high end of all rafters rests on a ledger beam permanently anchored to the terrace wall face. This is the critical structural connection.

  • Ledger beam: Eucalyptus 12-15cm diameter, ~6-7m length
  • Height: ~4.5m (ridge height minus one rafter depth)
  • Mounting: 5-6 steel plates with M12 stainless chemical anchors

Purlins

  • Quantity: 3-4 purlins, 8-10cm diameter
  • Arrangement: Semicircular arcs parallel to curved wall
  • Attachment: Lashed and pegged to rafters

Living Roof

Same construction as Standard BioDome:

  1. Sheathing: Cork board 30mm over purlins
  2. Waterproofing: EPDM liner 1mm, 15cm overlaps
  3. Drainage: Drainage mat + filter fabric
  4. Growing medium: 10-15cm lightweight substrate
  5. Planting: Sedums and drought-tolerant groundcovers

Wall Junction Waterproofing

The most critical detail in this design

Most Important Detail

The junction where the new roof meets the existing terrace wall is the most vulnerable point for water infiltration. Water entering here leaks into the building AND undermines the historic wall. This is the most important weatherproofing detail in the entire Terraced BioDome design.

Ledger Board Installation (8 Steps)

1

Mark Ledger Height

Ridge height (5.0m) minus one rafter depth (0.4-0.5m) = ledger at ~4.5m. Use level and chalk line to mark across wall face.

2

Mark Anchor Points

Mark anchor points every 1.2m along chalk line. Center each steel plate (150√ó150√ó6mm) over anchor point.

3

Core-Drill Anchor Holes

16mm diamond core drill, 200mm deep into solid stone only (not mortar joints). Requires professional equipment.

4

Clean Out Dust

Blow out all dust using hand pump (not breath). Hole must be completely clean for chemical anchor to cure properly.

5

Inject Chemical Anchor

Inject epoxy from bottom of hole upward, filling completely. Epoxy should squeeze out at surface.

6

Set Threaded Rod

Insert M12 stainless rod into wet epoxy with slow rotation. Do not disturb for 24 hours (longer if cold).

7

Test First Anchor

After full cure, apply 50 N¬∑m torque with torque wrench. Rod should not rotate. If it does, rework location.

8

Bolt Ledger in Place

Lift ledger onto steel plates. Bolt with stainless washers and nuts. Tighten gradually. Verify level.

Counter-Flashing Detail (5 Layers)

  1. Layer 1 ‚Äì Reglet: Horizontal groove 15mm deep √ó 100mm wide cut into stone, 100mm above ledger
  2. Layer 2 ‚Äì Counter-flashing: Lead or aluminum, 200mm tall, upper edge in reglet, lower edge with 45¬∞ drip lip
  3. Layer 3 ‚Äì Sealant: Polyurethane in reglet, tooled smooth
  4. Layer 4 ‚Äì Step flashing: EPDM 300√ó300mm squares, 100mm overlap, shingled pattern
  5. Layer 5 ‚Äì Main membrane: EPDM roof liner overlaps step flashing by 150mm
Mandatory Maintenance

The polyurethane sealant WILL crack within 5-7 years due to pole movement and UV exposure. This is expected.

  • Year 1: Inspect after first winter rains
  • Years 2-5: Annual visual inspection each autumn
  • Year 5-7: Re-apply polyurethane sealant completely
  • Ongoing: Re-seal every 5 years. Budget ‚Ç¨50-100/cycle.

Drainage Behind Wall

  • Do NOT seal weep holes. Keep them clear and functioning
  • Maintain 50mm gap at building base where foundation meets wall
  • Slope interior floor away from wall (minimum 1:50)
  • Install French drain if needed for evidence of water backing up

Construction Phases

14-16 week timeline

0

Phase 0: Wall Assessment & Preparation (Weeks 1-2)

  • Complete structural survey of terrace wall
  • Clean wall face of vegetation
  • Repoint loose mortar joints with hydraulic lime mortar
  • Mark ledger line at ~4.5m height
  • Core-drill anchor holes (5-6 holes)
  • Inject chemical anchors, allow 24-48hr cure
1

Phase 1: Pole Harvest & Treatment (Weeks 2-5)

  • Fell ~60 eucalyptus trees
  • Debark within 48 hours
  • Set up borax treatment tank
  • Dry under cover 2-3 weeks

See: Pole Treatment Protocol

2

Phase 2: Foundation Arc (Weeks 4-6)

  • Excavate semicircular trench (4.5m radius)
  • Tie arc ends into terrace wall base
  • Assemble gabion cages and fill with stone
  • Allow 1 week settling
3

Phase 3: Wall Framework (Weeks 6-7)

  • Char and oil 16 main posts
  • Set posts at ~1.2m spacing around arc
  • Weave horizontal wattle (~98 poles)
4

Phase 4: Ring Beam & Ledger (Weeks 7-8)

  • Install ledger on terrace wall (8 steps above)
  • Install curved ring beam on front wall top
  • Connect ring beam ends to ledger with brackets
5

Phase 5: Lean-to Roof (Weeks 8-9)

  • Lift 14-15 rafters into position, spacing 1.2m
  • Spike and lash all connections
  • Install purlins across rafters
6

Phase 6: Critical Waterproofing (Weeks 9-10)

  • Cut reglet into terrace wall
  • Install counter-flashing and seal
  • Install step flashing (EPDM squares)
  • Install cork sheathing and main EPDM membrane
  • Water test for 1 hour‚Äîdo not proceed until dry
7

Phase 7: Wall Infill & Finishing (Weeks 10-16)

  • Mix clay slip, coat straw, pack into cavities
  • Allow 4-6 weeks drying
  • Apply lime render (scratch coat + finish coat)
  • Install windows, doors, floor

Floor Plan (24m¬≤)

The 24m¬≤ floor is compact but efficient. The straight back wall (terrace) simplifies furniture placement.

Suggested Zones

  • Entry (2m¬≤): At one end of curved front, near kitchen for deliveries
  • Living Area (8m¬≤): Center of curved front, maximizing south-facing light
  • Kitchen Zone (4m¬≤): Adjacent to entry, compact galley layout
  • Sleeping Area (6m¬≤): Against terrace wall for thermal mass benefit
  • Bathroom (3m¬≤): Corner between kitchen and sleeping (shared plumbing)
  • Storage (1m¬≤): Nooks along terrace wall

Design Benefits

  • Thermal mass: Terrace wall absorbs solar heat during day, releases at night
  • Light: Curved front maximizes southern exposure
  • Accessibility: Single-story, no stairs
  • Efficiency: Compact footprint reduces heating load

Maintenance Schedule

Critical: Junction Re-sealing

  • Every 5-7 years: Re-apply polyurethane sealant at reglet. Remove old cracked sealant completely first. Budget ‚Ç¨50-100.

Seasonal

  • Autumn: Inspect wall junction before rainy season. Check for cracks in sealant.
  • Spring: Repair any winter water damage to render.
  • Summer: Maintain defensible space. Check weep holes are clear.

Long-term

  • Every 5-10 years: Reapply linseed oil to exposed timber
  • At 15-20 years: Inspect wire lashings, replace if corroded
  • At 30-40 years: Assess timber condition for replacement
