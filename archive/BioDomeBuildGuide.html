Part One: Universal Principles

Chapter 1: Why This Matters

Picture this: You're standing in what should be a diverse Mediterranean oak forest in Central Portugal‚Äîhome to cork oaks, stone pines, wild olive, and strawberry trees humming with life. Instead, you're surrounded by a silent eucalyptus monoculture. The ground is bare. Nothing grows beneath these Australian invaders.

This is the reality across 26% of Portuguese forests‚Äîover 800,000 hectares conquered by Eucalyptus globulus, the Tasmanian blue gum.

The Eucalyptus Problem
  • Drinks 20-30 liters of water per day (stolen from the watershed)
  • Releases allelopathic chemicals that poison other plants
  • Drops volatile oil-rich bark that turns landscapes into tinderboxes
  • Acidifies soil and destroys beneficial microbes
  • Creates biological deserts where rich ecosystems once thrived

The Radical Idea

What if the act of removing eucalyptus could produce something valuable? What if ecological restoration could be economically viable? What if "waste" biomass became functional architecture?

Enter the Regenerative BioDome‚Äîa permanent, fire-resistant home built from the very trees poisoning your land.

This Project Is

90% ecological restoration, 10% building project. You're not just constructing a home. You're healing a watershed, preventing wildfires, enabling native forest regeneration, and demonstrating that restoration can create real value.

How This Design Was Born

The BioDome concept emerged from a surprisingly humble beginning: a wattle fence. During a visit from family, we learned the traditional technique of weaving young eucalyptus poles into a fence to keep wild boars from our garden. As the weaving progressed, an experiment began‚Äîangling the poles at 45 degrees instead of the traditional 90. The structure became stronger, more dynamic.

From there, it was a small conceptual leap to imagine this trellis wrapping back on itself, forming a giant inverted wicker basket. This is biomimetic engineering: working with the material's nature rather than against it.

The Da Vinci Bridge: Understanding Reciprocal Framing

But wattle weaving is only half the story. The BioDome's roof uses a different structural principle‚Äîone that Leonardo da Vinci sketched over 500 years ago.

Around 1480, Leonardo designed a portable military bridge for rapid deployment. The genius was in what it didn't need: no nails, no rope, no fasteners of any kind. The structure holds itself together through reciprocal framing‚Äîeach beam rests on the next, and the geometry locks them in place under load. The more weight you add, the stronger it becomes.

This principle‚Äîwhere each element both supports and is supported by its neighbors‚Äîis what makes the BioDome's roof possible. A spiral of rafters, each resting on the one before it, creates a self-supporting dome with no central post. The technique appears in 12th-century Buddhist temples (Todai-ji in Nara, Japan) and traditional roundhouses across cultures, but Leonardo's bridge remains the clearest demonstration of why it works.

Friends built this da Vinci bridge over our stream using eucalyptus poles. Standing on it, you can feel how the structure responds‚Äîhow your weight presses the beams more firmly into their interlocking positions rather than pulling them apart. This is the same principle that holds the BioDome's reciprocal roof together, scaled up and arranged in a spiral.

Why Reciprocal Framing Matters

Traditional roof construction requires either a central post (blocking your floor space) or heavy ridge beams and complex joinery. Reciprocal framing eliminates both. The roof supports itself, leaving the interior completely open‚Äîand it can be built with round poles that would be difficult to use in conventional construction. Perfect for eucalyptus.

The eucalyptus invasion presented itself not just as a problem to be solved, but as a building material waiting to be harvested. If fire is inevitable in Mediterranean landscapes, the wisdom of indigenous peoples who lived harmoniously with fire becomes relevant: build structures that are quick, cheap, and fire-resistant‚Äîfrom the very fuel that feeds the flames.

Note on Precision Joinery

While eucalyptus is ideal for structural framing (posts, wattle poles, rafters, purlins), its tendency to warp and twist as it dries makes it unsuitable for precision millwork. Window frames, door frames, and mullions require dimensional stability for tight weatherseals and smooth operation. For these applications, use chestnut (Castanea sativa)‚Äîa native Mediterranean hardwood with excellent stability, natural rot resistance from high tannin content, and a long tradition of use in Portuguese building.

Chapter 2: Eucalyptus Harvesting & Eradication (EXPANDED)

Critical Understanding

This is an eradication project. The goal is to permanently kill the eucalyptus trees and enable native forest recovery. Every stump must be treated to prevent regrowth.

Cut-Stump Treatment (95-98% Success) ‚Äî RECOMMENDED METHOD

This is the gold standard for eucalyptus eradication and is what we recommend for most builds:

1

Cut at Ground Level

Fell the tree as close to ground as possible using a chainsaw. Make the cut clean and horizontal.

2

Treat Stump Immediately

Within 15 minutes: Apply 25-50% glyphosate solution or 15-25% triclopyr solution to the entire cut surface. Eucalyptus tissue seals quickly‚Äîyou must apply before the stump calluses over.

Application methods by terrain:

  • Squeeze bottle with flip-top (recommended for slopes): 500ml bottle clips to belt with carabiner. One-handed operation‚Äîflip open, squeeze onto stump, flip closed. Hands stay free for balance. No open containers.
  • Gel formulation: Triclopyr gel (e.g., Pathfinder II) won't spill even if dropped, clings to vertical cuts, works in light rain. More expensive but ideal for steep terrain.
  • Dauber bottle: Built-in sponge applicator like nail polish. Good for small stumps, very controlled, but slow for large cuts.
  • Paintbrush + jar: Flat terrain only. Spill hazard, requires two hands‚Äîavoid on slopes.
3

Harvest Poles While Fresh

During the 1-2 week window while the wood is still workable, harvest poles from the freshly fallen tree. Young poles (2-7 year growth) are ideal for building.

4

Monitor for Resprouting

Check all treated stumps monthly for 12-18 months. Immediately cut and treat any resprouting shoots.

Success rate: 95-98%. Cost per tree: ‚Ç¨0.50-2. Time commitment: One intensive harvest period, then monitoring.

Herbicide Details

Glyphosate (Non-selective, most common)

  • Active ingredient: Glyphosate isopropylamine
  • Concentration for stump treatment: 25-50% (concentrated formulations, NOT lawn herbicide)
  • How it works: Absorbed through freshly cut wood, translocates to roots, kills the whole plant
  • Cost: ‚Ç¨3-8 per liter (concentrated)
  • Weathering: Rain-resistant after 1-2 hours
  • Toxicity: Low mammalian toxicity, EPA approved, widely used

Triclopyr (Selective for woody plants)

  • Active ingredient: Triclopyr butoxyethyl ester
  • Concentration for stump treatment: 15-25%
  • How it works: Absorbed through wood, acts like plant hormone causing uncontrolled growth, plant dies
  • Cost: ‚Ç¨8-15 per liter (concentrated)
  • Weathering: Works even in wet conditions
  • Toxicity: Moderate, more expensive but highly effective

Safety Protocols (Non-Negotiable)

Personal Protective Equipment
  • Gloves: Nitrile or latex (herbicides absorb through skin)
  • Eye protection: Safety glasses or goggles
  • Skin coverage: Long sleeves, long pants (prevent accidental exposure)
  • Footwear: Closed-toe boots (protect feet from splashes)
  • Respiratory protection: Not typically needed for stump treatment (low volatility)

Timing and Weather Conditions

  • Best season: Late spring through autumn (trees are actively growing)
  • Avoid: Heavy rain within 2 hours (wash off herbicide), extreme heat (speeds evaporation)
  • Application time: Early morning or evening (cooler, minimizes volatility)
  • Dry period needed: At least 1-2 hours after application before rain

Environmental Justification for Herbicide Use

Some builders resist herbicides on principle, preferring "organic" methods. However, for eucalyptus eradication at landscape scale, herbicides are the most environmentally responsible choice:

  • Landscape health: Without herbicide treatment, eucalyptus resprouting creates denser, harder-to-remove thickets. Multiple years of cutting (without treatment) causes more disturbance than one chemical application.
  • Herbicide impact: Glyphosate and triclopyr are broad-spectrum (kill most plants) but break down rapidly in soil. Concentrations used for stump treatment are highly localized (applied to stumps, not broadcast).
  • Cost of alternatives: Physical removal (machinery, labor) causes massive soil compaction and erosion. Chemical spot treatment is gentler on soil.
  • Permanence: 95-98% success with chemical treatment means native forest can establish. Repeated cutting without herbicide means eucalyptus keeps coming back for decades.

Chapter 4: Pole Treatment Protocol

The 5-step process is identical across all three designs and is absolutely non-negotiable. Untreated eucalyptus fails in 5-10 years. Properly treated timber lasts 30-40 years above ground (expect partial timber replacement at this interval).

Eucalyptus Species Selection

For structural poles, select E. globulus (blue gum) or E. grandis (flooded gum). These species have characteristic bending strength (fm,k) of 25+ MPa, sufficient for the spans in this design. Avoid E. camaldulensis (river red gum) for primary rafters‚Äîits strength is more variable. Source poles 15-18cm diameter at the butt end for main rafters.

This is not optional

Skip pole treatment and your building will be colonized by carpenter ants within 3 years. Follow this protocol and your timber will last 30-40 years‚Äîlong enough to establish native forest around your home and plan for eventual timber replacement.

1

Debark Within 48 Hours

Remove bark immediately after felling while wood is fresh. Use a drawknife or scraper. Bark traps moisture and prevents preservative penetration. Expose bare wood completely.

2

Borax Soak (72+ Hours)

Mix borax and boric acid in ratio 12:8:80 (borax:boric acid:water by weight). Submerge poles completely‚Äîthey float, so weight them down. Minimum 72 hours; longer (up to 2 weeks) is better. This penetrates sapwood and creates a toxic barrier against insects and fungi.

3

Dry 2-3 Weeks Under Cover

Stack treated poles on raised supports in a shaded, well-ventilated area. Protect from rain. Air circulation is essential‚Äîpoles will mold in a closed space. Stack with spacers between layers.

4

Char Bottom 50cm (Deep Char Method)

Before installation, torch the lower 50cm of each pole (embedding portion) until the char layer reaches 8-10mm depth. This is deeper than cosmetic charring‚Äîyou should see alligator-scale cracking in the char layer. Traditional Japanese shou sugi ban uses similar depths. The deep char provides genuine rot resistance at the critical ground-contact zone. After charring, brush off loose char with a stiff wire brush.

Char Depth Matters

Shallow 3-5mm char is primarily cosmetic. For ground contact, you need 8-10mm to create a durable barrier. Test depth by scraping with a knife‚Äîchar should be difficult to penetrate.

5

Seal with Hot Linseed Oil

Heat linseed oil to 60-70¬∞C (warm to touch but not steaming). Brush on all pole surfaces. The oil hardens over 2-3 days and waterproofs the wood while remaining breathable. Reapply every 5-10 years.

Chapter 5: Wall Infill: Light Straw-Clay

Why Light Straw-Clay?

Traditional cob (dense clay-sand-straw mixture) works well for monolithic walls, but presents challenges when used as infill between a wooden frame‚Äîespecially with eucalyptus poles that shrink 5-8% as they dry over the first year.

The Problem with Cob Infill

Cob is rigid and heavy (~1,800 kg/m¬≥). When eucalyptus poles shrink and shift during drying, cob cracks at the pole junctions‚Äîsometimes severely. Repairs are time-consuming and the cracks often return.

Light straw-clay solves this by reversing the ratio: instead of clay with straw reinforcement, it's straw with clay coating. The result is a lightweight (~400 kg/m¬≥), flexible infill that:

  • Absorbs frame movement internally rather than cracking
  • Dries faster (4-6 weeks vs 2-3 months for cob)
  • Easier to repair (stuff more straw into gaps)
  • More forgiving to mix (less skill required for consistency)
  • Reduces load on frame (1/4 the weight of cob)

The Recipe

Ingredient Description Purpose
Straw Loose, long-fiber (wheat, rye, or rice straw) Bulk material‚Äîcreates insulation and structure
Clay slip Clay + water to thick cream/yogurt consistency Coating‚Äîbinds straw, provides fire resistance

No sand. Unlike cob, light straw-clay uses pure clay slip without aggregate. The straw provides all the structure.

Making Clay Slip

1

Soak Clay Overnight

Put clay soil in a container, cover with water. Let sit 12-24 hours to fully hydrate and break down clumps.

2

Mix to Yogurt Consistency

Stir vigorously (a paint mixer on a drill works well). Add water until the slip coats your hand and drips off slowly‚Äîlike thick yogurt or heavy cream.

3

Strain if Needed

If your clay soil has stones or debris, strain through a coarse screen. Pure clay slip coats straw evenly.

Coating the Straw

1

Toss Straw in Slip

Work in manageable batches. Toss loose straw into slip and turn it with your hands or a pitchfork until all fibers are lightly coated. The straw should glisten but not drip.

2

Don't Saturate

More slip is NOT better. Saturated straw takes longer to dry and loses insulation value. Every fiber should be coated, but the straw should still feel like straw‚Äînot like wet clay.

Packing the Wall Cavity

  1. Work between wattle poles in 15-20cm lifts
  2. Pack coated straw firmly into the cavity
  3. Tamp with a flat board or your fist‚Äîcompress to remove large air pockets
  4. Don't over-compress (you want insulation value, not density)
  5. Continue until cavity is filled to the next wattle level
  6. Final wall thickness: 35-45cm (slightly thinner than cob is acceptable due to better insulation value)
Tamping Technique

Tamp firmly but not aggressively. You want the straw to hold together and fill gaps, but over-tamping crushes the straw fibers and reduces insulation. The packed wall should feel springy, not solid.

Drying Time

  • Per 15cm depth: 1-2 weeks to dry through
  • Total wall drying: 4-6 weeks (much faster than cob due to porosity)
  • Test for dryness: Push a thin stick into the wall‚Äîif it comes out damp, keep waiting
  • Good ventilation: Essential during drying. Keep windows open, use fans if available

Light straw-clay's porous structure allows moisture to escape from all surfaces simultaneously, unlike dense cob which dries slowly from outside to inside.

Thermal Properties

Insulation vs Thermal Mass

Light straw-clay is insulation, not thermal mass. It resists heat flow rather than storing heat. Your thermal mass comes from the earthen floor (already included in all designs) and the gabion stone foundation. For passive solar sites, consider adding an interior cob bench or feature wall if additional heat storage is desired.

Fire Resistance

Clay-coated straw is fire-resistant‚Äîthe clay coating prevents the straw from igniting easily. In fire tests, light straw-clay chars on the surface but doesn't sustain flame. Combined with lime render, it provides excellent fire protection.

Repairs and Adjustments

One of light straw-clay's advantages is easy repair:

  • Small gaps from settling: Stuff with more clay-coated straw
  • Gaps at pole junctions: Pack additional straw around poles after they've finished shrinking (typically after first year)
  • Cracks in render: Standard lime render repair (documented in Finishing section)

Unlike cob repairs which require matching the original mix and careful re-bonding, light straw-clay repairs are nearly invisible once rendered.

Chapter 6: Fire Safety & Native Restoration

Structure Fire Resistance

All BioDome designs have exceptional fire resistance due to their construction materials:

  • Gabion stone foundation: Rock in wire mesh‚Äîcompletely non-combustible
  • Light straw-clay walls: Clay-coated straw chars but doesn't sustain flame‚Äîthe clay coating prevents ignition
  • Living roof: Growing plants hold moisture. Soil and vegetation absorb heat without igniting

The only combustible elements are window/door frames and structural poles, but these are fully enclosed within the fire-resistant envelope. Fire would need to breach stone, clay-coated straw walls, and living roof before reaching any wood.

Optional: Stone Apron for Enhanced Fire Protection

In high-risk fire zones, consider adding a stone apron to the lower portion of walls. This creates a non-combustible barrier at ground level‚Äîwhere embers accumulate and ground fires make first contact.

Design

  • Height: 50-100cm from foundation top
  • Materials: Local stone (same granite used in gabions), dry-stacked or lime-mortared
  • Thickness: 10-15cm facing, tied into wall with galvanized wire or stone anchors
  • Upper wall: Standard lime render above stone apron

Construction Options

  • Dry-stacked: Flat stones stacked without mortar, gravity-held. Allows wall to breathe fully. Easier to repair.
  • Lime-mortared: Stones set in lime mortar (NOT cement‚Äîlime remains breathable). More permanent, better ember-sealing.
  • Gabion extension: Continue the gabion cage upward 50-100cm before transitioning to straw-clay. Uses existing technique and materials.
Why Not Full Stone Cladding?

Stone on the entire wall adds significant weight (stressing the eucalyptus frame), reduces breathability (risking moisture problems), and increases cost substantially. The lower 50-100cm addresses the highest-risk zone while preserving the benefits of light straw-clay above.

Native Forest Restoration: The Long-Term Fire Strategy

The most effective fire prevention is replacing eucalyptus with native forest. Eucalyptus is explosively flammable. Native Atlantic forests don't burn the same way.

Your BioDome project is part of this restoration. Every eucalyptus you harvest for construction is one less fire ladder. Every native tree you plant in its place is long-term fire insurance. Within 10-15 years, the replanted zone becomes a living firebreak.

Defensible Space Zones

Zone 2 5-30m Mowed grass, no eucalyptus, maintained fire breaks. Young native trees encouraged.

Replanting Priorities

Focus on fire-resistant natives: Quercus robur (pedunculate oak), Quercus suber (cork oak), Castanea sativa (sweet chestnut), and Arbutus unedo (strawberry tree). These hold moisture, don't produce flammable oils, and create closed canopy that shades out eucalyptus regrowth.

Rafter Sizing Check

For the Standard BioDome reciprocal roof (4.5m effective span, 1.5 kN/m¬≤ design load), a 15cm diameter rafter experiences approximately 30 MPa bending stress. This is at the limit for many eucalyptus species. Do not use poles smaller than 15cm butt diameter for main rafters. Reject poles with excessive curve, spiral grain, or visible defects.

Connection Design Notes

Spike selection: Use 316 stainless steel for all primary structural connections. Eucalyptus heartwood is acidic (pH 3.5-4.5) and contains extractives that accelerate galvanized steel corrosion. Hot-dip galvanized spikes in exterior eucalyptus applications may lose their zinc coating within 15-25 years.

Notch requirement: At reciprocal roof overlaps, cut a 25-30mm deep saddle notch into the supporting rafter. Friction and spike connections alone are insufficient to resist asymmetric wind loads. The notch provides mechanical interlock.

Wire lashing: Galvanized wire lashing provides redundancy but is secondary to the spike and notch. Pre-tension lashing tight and inspect annually for first 5 years.

Foundation Notes for Mediterranean Climate

The gabion ring foundation is substantially oversized for bearing capacity (actual bearing pressure ~3-4 kN/m¬≤ vs. allowable 100+ kN/m¬≤). This is intentional‚Äîthe gabion serves primarily as a drainage layer, leveling base, and moisture break, not as a high-capacity footing.

Frost heave: Not a significant concern in Central Portugal. The 30cm below-grade depth is adequate.

Capillary break: Add an HDPE slip sheet or gravel drainage layer beneath the ring beam to prevent moisture wicking into post bases. This is more important than frost depth in Mediterranean climates.

Reciprocal Ring Beam

The ring beam uses the reciprocal principle‚Äîeach pole rests on its neighbor and carries the next one in sequence. This dates to 12th century Buddhist temple construction (Todai-ji in Nara, Japan).

  • Number of poles: 24 (matching wall posts below)
  • Pole diameter: 12-15cm eucalyptus
  • Span: Each pole spans approximately 2 post spacings
  • Overlap: ~50cm where each pole rests on the previous one
  • Fastening: 125mm stainless steel spike at each overlap (316 grade recommended‚Äîeucalyptus is acidic and accelerates galvanized corrosion), plus galvanized wire lashing for redundancy
  • Stability: Even unbound, the geometry creates mutual support

Reciprocal Roof

  • Main rafters: 12 poles, 15-18cm butt diameter, 5.5m length. Use E. globulus or E. grandis for adequate bending strength (fm,k ‚â• 25 MPa). Reject poles with significant curve, spiral grain, or diameter less than 15cm at the butt.
  • Purlins: Smaller poles (8-10cm) spanning between rafters
  • Central opening: ~1m diameter for skylight/ventilation
  • Roof angle: Controlled by the "rummy" (horizontal distance between connection points)

Central Opening Waterproofing (Critical Detail)

The ~1m diameter central opening is the lowest point of the reciprocal roof spiral. Without proper detailing, water from the living roof drains toward this opening.

  • Raised curb: Install a 150mm high curb around the opening using cork or treated timber. EPDM membrane wraps up and over this curb.

  • Drainage relief: Cut 4 small notches (25mm wide √ó 10mm deep) in the curb at cardinal points to allow trapped water to escape to the main roof drainage layer.

  • Outlet height: Set scupper inlet 50mm above normal drainage layer level‚Äîwater only exits when drainage is overwhelmed.
  • Discharge: Direct overflow away from foundation via splash blocks or gravel drainage strip.

Reciprocal Roof Assembly: Step-by-Step

The reciprocal roof is the most technically demanding aspect of the Standard BioDome. This section provides the detailed guidance needed to execute it safely and successfully.

Ground Rehearsal (Critical First Step)

Why Ground Rehearsal Matters
  • Reveals pole fit problems before you're working at height
  • Numbers poles for correct assembly sequence
  • Trains your crew on the spiral geometry
  • Takes 2-3 hours but can save days of frustration

Process:

  1. Clear a flat area minimum 12m x 12m
  2. Lay first rafter on ground, thick end pointing clockwise
  3. Place second rafter across first, ~50cm from thin end of first
  4. Continue spiral pattern: each pole rests on the previous one
  5. Mark overlap positions with chalk on each pole
  6. Number poles 1-12 in sequence (use paint or tags)
  7. Note any poles that don't fit well‚Äîswap or trim before raising

Temporary Center Support Tower

The "chicken-and-egg" problem: reciprocal frames are self-supporting once complete, but during assembly, early rafters have nothing to rest on at the center. Solution: build a temporary tower.

Tower Construction:

  • Materials: 4 eucalyptus poles, 2.5m length, 10-15cm diameter
  • Assembly: Square tower with diagonal cross-bracing at 3 levels
  • Height: Top platform at final roof center height (~4.5m from floor)
  • Platform: Small plywood sheet (60x60cm) or crossed poles at top
  • Load capacity: Must support first 4-5 rafters (~150-200kg combined)
Safety Check

Before raising any rafters, test the tower by having someone climb to the platform. If it wobbles or deflects noticeably, add more bracing. The tower must remain stable when supporting offset loads.

Crew Positions and Choreography

Minimum crew: 6 people

Position Count Location Responsibility

Raising Sequence

  1. Rafter 1: Lift thick end to ring beam, seat on post notch. Thin end rests on center tower platform.
  2. Rafter 2: Same process. Overlap thin end on Rafter 1, approximately 50cm from Rafter 1's tip.
  3. Rafters 3-4: Complete first quadrant. Each overlaps previous at thin end.
  4. Rafter 5: First pole that overlaps TWO others (Rafter 4 + starts passing over the pattern)
  5. Rafters 6-11: Continue spiral. Each new rafter overlaps the previous one only.
  6. Rafter 12 (closure): The tricky final piece‚Äîsee dedicated section below.

The Closure Move (Rafter 12)

Critical Step

Rafter 12 is the most challenging moment. It must simultaneously slide under Rafter 1's thin end while resting on top of Rafter 11. Take your time and don't force it.

Technique:

  1. Position crew: 2 people on Rafter 1, 2 lifting Rafter 12, 1 guiding at center
  2. Lift Rafter 1's thin end 10-15cm using a temporary block or lever
  3. Slide Rafter 12 underneath Rafter 1's thin end
  4. Position Rafter 12 so it rests on Rafter 11
  5. Slowly lower Rafter 1 until it settles onto Rafter 12
  6. The geometry locks in place‚Äîverify all connections are seated

If it doesn't fit: Do NOT force it. Lower Rafter 1 completely and reassess. Common issues:

  • Rafter 12 is too short‚Äîswap for a longer pole
  • Overlap point is wrong‚Äîadjust position on Rafter 11
  • Rafter 1 has shifted‚Äîreposition before attempting closure again

Fastening Sequence

Wait Until Complete

Do NOT fasten any connections until all 12 rafters are positioned and the complete geometry is verified. Premature fastening can trap misalignments.

  1. Verify geometry: Check all overlaps are approximately 50cm from thin ends. The spiral should appear uniform.
  2. Start at Rafter 1-2 overlap: This is your reference point.
  3. Pre-drill: Use 6mm drill bit through both poles to prevent splitting.
  4. Drive spike: 125mm stainless steel spike (316 grade) through pre-drilled hole. Avoid galvanized‚Äîeucalyptus is acidic (pH 3.5-4.5) and accelerates zinc corrosion.
  5. Wire lash: Wrap galvanized wire around the connection as redundancy.
  6. Move clockwise: Proceed to Rafter 2-3 overlap, then continue full circle.
  7. Complete all 12 connections before removing center support.

Removing Center Support

After all 12 connections are fastened:

  1. Final inspection: confirm every spike is driven and every wire lashing is tight
  2. Have crew ready to observe the structure for any movement
  3. Remove the platform from top of tower first
  4. Lower tower poles one at a time, starting with non-load-bearing corners
  5. The structure should remain completely stable
  6. If any settling or creaking occurs, STOP and reinspect connections

Waterproofing at Wall Junction - Critical Detail

The junction where the new roof meets the existing terrace wall is the most vulnerable point for water infiltration. Water entering here will not only leak into the building‚Äîit will also percolate behind the roof and undermine the historic wall. This is the most important weatherproofing detail in the entire Terraced BioDome design.

Ledger Board Installation

The ledger is a horizontal structural beam that sits high on the terrace wall face and carries the high end of all roof rafters. It must be permanently anchored into solid stone.

Components:

  • Ledger beam: Eucalyptus pole 12‚Äì15cm diameter, length = building width + 0.5m overhang at each end (~6‚Äì7m total)
  • Steel mounting plates: 150√ó150√ó6mm pre-drilled steel, spaced every 1.2m (typically 5‚Äì6 plates)
  • Chemical anchors: M12 stainless steel threaded rods with epoxy/resin injection cartridges
  • Washers and nuts: Stainless M12 to prevent corrosion

Installation Steps (8 critical steps):

1

Mark Ledger Height

Calculate: Ridge height (5.0m) minus one rafter depth (0.4‚Äì0.5m) = ledger sits at ~4.5m. Use a level and chalk line to mark this height across the wall face.

2

Mark Anchor Points

Starting from one end, mark anchor points every 1.2m along the chalk line. Center each steel plate over its anchor point. Mark hole centers with a pencil.

3

Core-Drill Anchor Holes

Using a 16mm diamond core drill, drill 200mm deep into solid stone only (not mortar joints). This requires professional equipment. Hole angle should be as perpendicular to wall face as possible. If wall is uneven, angle slightly downward.

4

Clean Out Dust

Blow out all dust and debris using a hand pump (not your breath‚Äîbreath introduces moisture). Flush hole with dry brush. Hole must be completely clean for chemical anchor to cure properly.

5

Inject Chemical Anchor

Follow manufacturer instructions. Typical process: Insert cartridge in caulking gun, inject epoxy from bottom of hole upward, filling completely. Epoxy should squeeze out at surface, confirming full fill. Do not stir‚Äîlet cartridge deliver consistent ratio.

6

Set Threaded Rod

Insert the M12 stainless rod into the wet epoxy with slow rotation to avoid trapping air. Push to correct depth (15mm threads should protrude for mounting plate). Do not disturb for 24 hours (or per manufacturer cure time; longer if temperature <15¬∞C).

7

Test First Anchor

After full cure, use a torque wrench to test the first anchor: Apply 50 N¬∑m torque. Rod should not rotate. If it does, reject that anchor and rework (may need deeper hole or new location).

8

Bolt Ledger in Place

Once all anchors are set and tested, lift ledger into position on steel plates. Bolt through with washers and stainless nuts. Tighten gradually around the ledger (not one side at a time, to avoid twisting). Verify level along full length with a long level.

Counter-Flashing Detail

Water running down the wall face must be intercepted and directed away from the junction. This is achieved through a series of overlapping flashing layers.

Five Layers (from wall surface outward):

  • Layer 1 ‚Äì Reglet (cut into stone): A horizontal groove 15mm deep √ó 100mm wide cut into the stone wall face, 100mm directly above the ledger beam. This groove must be cut precisely horizontal using a stone saw or mason's chisel. Depth must reach solid stone, not mortar.
  • Layer 2 ‚Äì Counter-flashing (lead or aluminum): A pre-formed or custom-bent flashing piece 200mm tall, with the upper edge bent 90¬∞ to fit into the reglet. The lower edge extends 100mm down and forms a 45¬∞ drip lip. Material: Lead (best, lasts 50+ years) or aluminum (acceptable, 20‚Äì30 years). Install by pressing upper edge into reglet, then seal with polyurethane sealant.
  • Layer 3 ‚Äì Sealant (polyurethane in reglet): After counter-flashing is in place, back-fill the reglet with polyurethane sealant, tooled smooth and slightly recessed. This prevents water from entering behind the flashing.
  • Layer 4 ‚Äì Step flashing (EPDM strips): Cut EPDM flashing into 300mm √ó 300mm squares. Starting at one end of the ledger, lay step flashing pieces with 100mm overlap. Each piece is half under the roof sheathing and half down the wall surface, creating a stepped pattern.
  • Layer 5 ‚Äì Main membrane overlap (EPDM roof liner): The main roof EPDM membrane is installed last and overlaps the step flashing by 150mm, creating a shingled effect. Water on the roof sheds onto the membrane, down the step flashing, onto the counter-flashing, and away from the building.
Mandatory Maintenance Schedule

The polyurethane sealant at the reglet WILL crack within 5-7 years due to eucalyptus pole movement (2-4% shrinkage), thermal cycling, and UV exposure. This is not a defect‚Äîit's the nature of sealing a moving joint to a rigid wall.

Required inspections:

  • Year 1: Inspect after first winter rains. Look for water staining below junction.
  • Years 2-5: Annual visual inspection each autumn before rainy season.
  • Year 5-7: Re-apply polyurethane sealant. Remove old cracked sealant completely before re-sealing.
  • Ongoing: Re-seal every 5 years as part of normal maintenance. Budget ‚Ç¨50-100 for materials each cycle.

Drainage Behind Wall

If the terrace wall has soil behind it (typical case), water pressure from rainfall and groundwater can accumulate. Your new building must not interfere with existing drainage or create dams.

Critical requirements:

  • Do NOT seal weep holes. Weep holes are intentional drainage points. If they exist, keep them clear and functioning. Add weep holes if none exist (drill 25mm holes, 2‚Äì3m spacing at base).
  • Maintain 50mm gap at building base. Where the building foundation meets the wall, leave a 50mm clear gap to allow water to drain down the wall face rather than backing up.
  • Slope interior floor to drain away from wall. The interior floor should slope at minimum 1:50 (2cm drop per meter) toward the curved front wall, not toward the terrace wall.
  • Install French drain if needed. If evidence of water backing up exists (wet soil, efflorescence), install a perforated drain pipe at the wall base running parallel to the wall, with outlet at low point away from building.

Anchor/Ledger Attachment to Stone - Engineering Notes

Chemical Anchor Specification

Traditional mechanical expansion anchors (like plastic wall plugs) risk cracking historic masonry and are not recommended. Chemical anchors‚Äîwhere epoxy or polyester resin encapsulates a threaded rod in the hole‚Äîare the standard for restoration work and permanent attachment to stone.

Product selection criteria:

  • Type: Two-part epoxy or vinyl ester injection system (cartridge format, not manual mixing)
  • Brands (European): Hilti HIT-RE 500 V3, Simpson SET-XP, Fischer FIS V, Sika AnchorFix, Fischerwerke Injektionsd√ºbel
  • Embedment depth: Minimum 150mm into solid stone (not mortar)
  • Rod type: M12 stainless steel (corrosion-resistant for longevity)
  • Cure time: Typically 24 hours at 20¬∞C. Longer at lower temperatures (36‚Äì48 hours if <10¬∞C).
  • Load rating: Verify that M12 stainless in your stone type achieves >15 kN pullout capacity. (Your roof load requires ~9 kN per anchor; use safety factor 2.5x.)

Quality control during installation:

  • Hole must be completely dust-free. Use a hand pump to blow out, or flush with brush and let dry.
  • Inject resin from bottom of hole upward to prevent air pockets.
  • Insert rod with slow rotation (10‚Äì20 rpm) to avoid trapping air.
  • Resin should squeeze out at surface‚Äîconfirms full fill.
  • Mark hole with date and do not touch for full cure time (24 hours minimum).
  • Test first anchor with torque wrench before proceeding with others.

Load Transfer Explanation

Understanding the forces helps explain why chemical anchors are sized as they are.

Roof load calculation:

  • Dead load: Living roof weight = ~15cm substrate at ~150 kg/m¬≥ = ~225 kg/m¬≤ (including drainage mat, EPDM, cork, rafters)
  • Live load: Maintenance access, snow, wind uplift = ~100 kg/m¬≤
  • Total design load: ~325 kg/m¬≤ (conservative)

Roof area and total force:

  • Roof area: ~30m¬≤ (semicircular at 25‚Äì30¬∞ slope = sloped area, not plan area)
  • Total roof load: 30m¬≤ √ó 325 kg/m¬≤ = ~9,750 kg = ~97.5 kN
  • Distributed to ledger anchors: Assume 8 anchor points = ~12 kN per anchor
  • Design load per anchor: 12 kN √ó 2.5 safety factor = 30 kN pullout required

Conclusion: M12 chemical anchors at 150mm embedment in competent stone (sandstone, granite) easily achieve 30+ kN. If stone is soft (limestone, chalk, mudstone) or heavily fractured, use M14 rods, increase embedment to 200mm, or reduce anchor spacing to 1.0m instead of 1.2m.

The north-facing back wall uses compressed straw bales (450mm thick):

  • R-value: R-30 (vs R-3.5 for fiberglass of same thickness)
  • Fire resistance: Dense straw won't burn (clay plaster coating prevents flame spread)
  • Breathability: Allows moisture to pass through
  • Assembly: 1 bale high + 50mm lime plaster on both sides

  • Purpose: Diffuse north light (no glare), stack-effect ventilation

  • Sunny day: 12-15kWh generation, 6kWh use, 6-9kWh stored in battery

  • Refrigerator: ~2.5kWh/day (non-negotiable)

  • Water heating (solar thermal + backup): ~2kWh/day
  • Internet/devices: ~0.3kWh/day

This works. But only if you're intentional about matching your use to the sun's availability.

The Solar Pod's rectangular footprint (5.0m √ó 4.1m) and sleeping loft create naturally distinct zones. Unlike the circular designs, the rectangular plan simplifies furniture placement and allows for efficient room divisions.

  • Clerestory on north wall captures light without summer overheating
Summer Ventilation Critical (Central Portugal)

Central Portugal routinely exceeds 40¬∞C in summer. The Solar Pod's thermal mass and insulation, designed for winter comfort, can trap heat dangerously without proper ventilation.

Required ventilation features:

  • Cross-ventilation: Install operable windows on both east and west walls (not just one side). Minimum 0.5m¬≤ openable area per wall.
  • Clerestory operation: The north clerestory must be operable, not fixed glazing. Summer nights: open fully for stack-effect cooling.
  • Loft vents: Add ventilated gable ends or soffit vents at loft level‚Äîhot air rises and needs an exit path above the sleeping area.
  • Thermal mass strategy: Open windows overnight to cool mass, close by 10am to retain coolness through the day.

For summer-only use, prioritize passive cooling over insulation. Consider external shading (vine pergola, shade cloth) on the south-facing solar array side.

Window & Door Weatherproofing (Critical Detail)

Window and door penetrations are the most common water entry points in natural buildings. Central Portugal's winter storms drive rain horizontally‚Äîproper flashing is essential.

Sill Pan (All Windows)

  • Slope: Window sills must slope outward at minimum 15¬∞ (1:4 ratio)
  • Pan liner: Install EPDM or metal pan liner beneath window frame, extending 50mm up frame jambs and 100mm beyond sill edge
  • Drip edge: Sill must project 40mm beyond wall face with undercut drip groove
  • End dams: Turn up pan liner at both ends to prevent water running along sill into wall

Head Flashing (All Openings)

  • Z-flashing: Install galvanized or aluminum Z-flashing above all window and door heads
  • Extension: Flashing must extend 50mm beyond frame on each side
  • Overlap: Lime render overlaps top leg of flashing; bottom leg overlaps frame head

Jamb Sealing

  • Backer rod: Install closed-cell foam backer rod in gap between frame and rough opening
  • Sealant: Apply polyurethane sealant over backer rod (not silicone‚Äîit doesn't adhere to lime)
  • Inspection: Re-seal jambs every 5-7 years as part of regular maintenance

Door Threshold

  • Elevation: Door threshold must be minimum 50mm above exterior grade
  • Drainage: Slope exterior surface away from threshold at 2% minimum
  • Weather strip: Install adjustable door sweep or threshold seal
Avoid Roundwood Frames for Exterior Openings

Roundwood (unprocessed poles) makes attractive interior trim but performs poorly at weathered exterior openings. The irregular surface creates gaps that are difficult to seal. Use kiln-dried, dimensioned timber (chestnut, oak, or treated softwood) for all window and door frames exposed to weather.

Interior Moisture Management

Natural buildings with breathable walls and living roofs require active moisture management. Without it, winter condensation leads to mold and accelerated decay.

Condensation Risk in Winter

Occupancy generates 2-4 kg of water vapor daily (breathing, cooking, bathing). In an unheated or minimally-heated off-grid structure, this moisture condenses on cold surfaces‚Äîespecially north-facing walls and the underside of the roof.

Ventilation Requirements

  • Continuous background ventilation: Install trickle vents in at least 2 windows (opposite walls for cross-flow). Target 0.5 air changes per hour minimum.
  • Extract ventilation: Bathroom and kitchen areas need extract fans vented to exterior‚Äîdo not rely on passive ventilation alone.
  • Winter protocol: Open windows briefly (5-10 minutes) each morning to flush humid air, then close to retain heat.
