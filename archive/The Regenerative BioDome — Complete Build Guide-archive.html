Part One: Understanding

Chapter 1: Why This Matters

Picture this: You're standing in what should be a diverse Mediterranean oak forest in Central Portugal‚Äîhome to cork oaks, stone pines, wild olive, and strawberry trees humming with life. Instead, you're surrounded by a silent eucalyptus monoculture. The ground is bare. Nothing grows beneath these Australian invaders.

This is the reality across 26% of Portuguese forests‚Äîover 800,000 hectares conquered by Eucalyptus globulus, the Tasmanian blue gum.

‚ö†Ô∏è The Eucalyptus Problem
  • Drinks 20-30 liters of water per day (stolen from the watershed)
  • Releases allelopathic chemicals that poison other plants
  • Drops volatile oil-rich bark that turns landscapes into tinderboxes
  • Acidifies soil and destroys beneficial microbes
  • Creates biological deserts where rich ecosystems once thrived

The Radical Idea

What if the act of removing eucalyptus could produce something valuable? What if ecological restoration could be economically viable? What if "waste" biomass became functional architecture?

Enter the Regenerative BioDome‚Äîa permanent, fire-resistant home built from the very trees poisoning your land.

You're not just constructing a home. You're healing a watershed, preventing wildfires, enabling native forest regeneration, and demonstrating that restoration can create real value.

How This Design Was Born

The BioDome concept emerged from a surprisingly humble beginning: a wattle fence.

During a visit from family, we learned the traditional technique of weaving young eucalyptus poles into a fence to keep wild boars from our garden. As the weaving progressed, an experiment began‚Äîangling the poles at 45 degrees instead of the traditional 90. The structure became stronger, more dynamic.

From there, it was a small conceptual leap to imagine this trellis wrapping back on itself, forming a giant inverted wicker basket. When you weave a basket, you want the young flexible poles‚Äî1 to 5 years old‚Äîbecause they bend easily into place and then dry in mutually supported tension. The structure shifts over time as each pole dries and twists individually, but the whole system has enough flex to accommodate this movement.

This is biomimetic engineering: working with the material's nature rather than against it.

The eucalyptus invasion presented itself not just as a problem to be solved, but as a building material waiting to be harvested. If fire is inevitable in Mediterranean landscapes, the wisdom of indigenous peoples who lived harmoniously with fire becomes relevant: build structures that are quick, cheap, and fire-resistant‚Äîfrom the very fuel that feeds the flames.

The BioDome design progressively evolved: first a simple woven structure, then weatherproofing with greenhouse plastic, then a proper gabion foundation to get the wood off the ground, then cob infill for thermal mass and fire resistance, and finally a living roof that completes the regenerative cycle. Each improvement addressed a specific limitation while maintaining the core principle: turn the problem into the solution.

Note on Precision Joinery

While eucalyptus is ideal for structural framing (posts, wattle poles, rafters, purlins), its tendency to warp and twist as it dries makes it unsuitable for precision millwork. Window frames, door frames, and mullions require dimensional stability for tight weatherseals and smooth operation. For these applications, use chestnut (Castanea sativa)‚Äîa native Mediterranean hardwood with excellent stability, natural rot resistance from high tannin content, and a long tradition of use in Portuguese building.

Chapter 4: Eucalyptus Harvesting & Eradication

üî• Critical Understanding

This is an eradication project. The goal is to permanently kill the eucalyptus trees and enable native forest recovery. Every stump must be treated to prevent regrowth.

Harvesting Protocol

1

Select Trees

Choose 2-7 year growth, 8-20cm diameter. Prioritize trees near water sources, competing with natives, or creating fire hazards.

2

Cut at Ground Level

Fell trees as close to ground as possible. Harvest poles immediately while fresh and flexible.

3

Treat Stump Immediately

Within 15 minutes: Paint cut stump surface with 25-50% glyphosate or triclopyr solution. This is critical‚Äîeucalyptus tissue seals quickly.

4

Monitor Monthly

Check all treated stumps monthly for 12-18 months. Immediately cut and retreat any sprouts.

Chapter 6: Wall Framework

The primary wall structure uses treated eucalyptus poles embedded in the gabion foundation, with horizontal poles woven between them to create the framework for cob infill.

Pole Treatment (Critical)

‚ö†Ô∏è All poles must be fully treated before use
  1. Debark within 48 hours of cutting
  2. Soak in borax/boric acid solution (12:8:80 ratio) for 72+ hours
  3. Dry for 2-3 weeks under cover
  4. Char bottom 50cm (embedding portion) with torch
  5. Seal with hot linseed oil

Vertical Poles

  • Quantity: 24 main posts
  • Diameter: 15-20cm
  • Length: 3.5m (embedded 40cm in gabion)
  • Spacing: ~1.2m around perimeter

Horizontal Poles

  • Quantity: ~100 pieces
  • Diameter: 5-8cm
  • Spacing: 25-30cm vertical gaps
  • Attachment: Lashed with galvanized wire + natural cordage

üí° The Drop Test

Form a ball of cob and drop from waist height. It should hold together without shattering (too dry) or flattening (too wet). Perfect cob slumps slightly but maintains shape.

Wall Building Process

  • Build in 30-40cm lifts, let each cure 1-2 days
  • Work around entire perimeter (even drying)
  • Interlock each lift with previous using thumb holes
  • Embed horizontal poles as you build
  • Final wall thickness: 40-50cm

Chapter 8: Reciprocal Ring Beam

The ring beam uses the same reciprocal principle as the roof above it‚Äîeach pole rests on its neighbor and carries the next one in sequence, creating a self-locking spiral that crowns the wall. This technique dates to 12th century Japan, where Buddhist monk Chogen developed spiral layering of wood for temple construction. The Great South Gate of Todai-ji in Nara remains a stunning example.

The Reciprocal Principle

Even without any fixing, a reciprocal frame can be stable‚Äîthe geometry itself creates mutual support. Each pole rests on the previous one approximately 50cm from its thinner end, forming a continuous spiral. The more poles in the ring, the more stable the unbound structure becomes. In actual construction we add fastening for safety, but this intrinsic stability is what makes the system so elegant.

Specifications

  • Number of poles: 24 (matching wall posts below)
  • Pole diameter: 12-15cm eucalyptus roundwood
  • Span: Each pole spans approximately 2 post spacings
  • Overlap: ~50cm where each pole rests on the previous
  • Fastening: Pre-drill and secure with 125mm galvanized spike at each overlap, plus wire lashing
Why Roundwood?

Round poles are stronger than square or rectangular timber of the same weight because the fiber structure is nowhere cut. The natural taper of eucalyptus poles (thicker at base, thinner at tip) also aids the spiral layering‚Äîthinner ends rest on thicker sections of the pole below.

Chapter 9: Reciprocal Roof

The reciprocal roof is a self-supporting structure where each rafter rests on the one before it, spiraling to a central opening. It requires no center post, distributes loads across all members simultaneously, and‚Äîcritically for cob walls‚Äîtransmits only vertical force to the walls below with no outward thrust.

Ground Simulation First

Before raising heavy beams above people's heads, lay out the frame on the ground. Bring in each rafter and lay it across the last one about 50cm from its thinner end, building up the spiral flat. This simulation reveals any fit problems and lets you number the poles for assembly sequence. Use lightweight material like bamboo first if you want to understand the geometry before committing.

Structure

  • Main rafters: 12 poles, 15-18cm diameter, 5.5m length
  • Purlins: Smaller poles (8-10cm) spanning between rafters
  • Central opening: ~1m diameter for skylight/ventilation
  • Roof angle: Controlled by the "rummy"‚Äîthe horizontal distance between consecutive connection points. Smaller rummy = steeper pitch.

Assembly Sequence

Following the spiral, raise each rafter individually onto its bearing point on the ring beam and the previous rafter. Pre-drill a hole through the pole into the rafter below, and drive a 125mm galvanized spike. The first few rafters need temporary support until enough are placed to lock the spiral. Once complete, the structure is remarkably stable‚Äîeach pole locked by the weight of those resting upon it.

For deeper study of reciprocal frame geometry and construction, see Reciprocal Frame Architecture by Olga Popovic Larsen, or visit Natural Building Blog's gallery of reciprocal roofs.

Chapter 11: Fire Safety

üî• Fire Danger Protocol

This is a region where wildfires are possible. Your BioDome is built to resist fire, but the surrounding landscape must be managed aggressively. Complacency kills.

Structure Fire Resistance

The BioDome has exceptional fire resistance due to its construction materials:

  • Gabion stone foundation: Rock in wire mesh‚Äîcompletely non-combustible. Fire cannot damage or ignite stone.
  • Cob walls: Unfired clay-sand-straw mixture. Clay is essentially fired earth‚Äîit cannot burn. The straw content is encased in clay and will not ignite.
  • Living roof: Growing plants hold moisture. The soil substrate and vegetation create a heat-absorbing, non-combustible layer.

The only combustible elements are the wooden door/window frames and the eucalyptus structural poles‚Äîbut these are fully enclosed within the non-combustible envelope of stone, cob, and living roof. Fire would need to breach these barriers before reaching any wood.

Native Forest Restoration: The Long-Term Fire Strategy

The most effective fire prevention is replacing eucalyptus with native forest. Eucalyptus is explosively flammable‚Äîvolatile oils in the leaves, shredding bark that carries fire up the trunk, and a tendency to drop burning branches. The catastrophic fires that have swept through Portugal and Galicia in recent decades burned almost exclusively through eucalyptus plantations.

Native Atlantic forests don't burn the same way. Oak, chestnut, and cork oak have high moisture content, don't produce volatile oils, and retain their leaves or bark in ways that resist ignition. A mature native forest is a firebreak, not a fire highway.

Your BioDome project is part of this restoration. Every eucalyptus you harvest for construction is one less fire ladder. Every native tree you plant in its place is long-term fire insurance. Within 10-15 years, the replanted zone becomes a living firebreak that protects not just your home but the entire landscape.

Zone Distance Treatment
Zone 1 0‚Äì5m Zero flammable vegetation. Mineral surface only (gravel, stone, bare earth). This is your immediate buffer against radiant heat and direct flame contact.
Zone 2 5‚Äì30m Mowed grass, no eucalyptus, maintained fire breaks. Young native trees (oak, chestnut, cork oak) are encouraged‚Äîthey will become your long-term protection as they mature.

üí° Replanting Priorities

Focus Zone 2 and Zone 3 replanting on fire-resistant natives: Quercus robur (pedunculate oak), Quercus suber (cork oak), Castanea sativa (sweet chestnut), and understory species like Arbutus unedo (strawberry tree). These species hold moisture, don't produce flammable oils, and create a closed canopy that shades out eucalyptus regrowth.
