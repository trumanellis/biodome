Part One: Understanding

Chapter 1: Why the Terraced BioDome Matters

Imagine an agricultural landscape with terrace walls built centuries ago‚Äîdry stone ramparts holding back hillsides, maintaining the slope. These walls represent human engineering at its best: elegant, enduring, integrated with the land.

Now imagine that same landscape invaded by eucalyptus. The walls remain, but they're now surrounded by water-stealing monoculture that blocks views, increases fire risk, and destroys the possibility of native forest return.

The Terraced BioDome is the solution for these specific landscapes. It's a compact, beautiful home that:

  • Uses existing terrace walls as structural anchors
  • Requires 50-60% fewer materials than a full-circle BioDome
  • Features simpler lean-to roof construction (no reciprocal complexity)
  • Adapts naturally to sloped terrain
  • Creates a defensible, fire-resistant residence
  • Removes invasive trees while preserving agricultural infrastructure

Like its circular sibling, the Terraced BioDome grew from a simple insight: the eucalyptus trees destroying our forests and fueling our fires can become the very material we build with. The design originated from experimenting with traditional wattle fencing techniques, angling young poles at 45 degrees to create stronger, more dynamic structures. The same principle of working with the material's nature‚Äîrather than against it‚Äîapplies here, adapted for the unique opportunities that terraced landscapes provide. (For the full origin story, see the Standard BioDome Guide.)

üí° This Design Is For You If
  • Your land has existing terrace walls or similar vertical stone structures
  • You want to minimize material quantities and cost
  • You're uncomfortable with complex reciprocal roof construction
  • You want faster, simpler building with beginner-friendly techniques
  • You prefer a half-circular or D-shaped footprint to a full circle

If your site doesn't have terrace walls but is otherwise suitable, you can adapt this guide to build against any existing stone wall structure‚Äîbarn walls, boundary walls, or historic dry stone features.

Design Principles

Site Adaptation: This design assumes a vertical stone wall or similar structure exists on your site. The terrace wall becomes the structural anchor, eliminating the need to build a complete ring foundation.

Material Efficiency: A semicircular footprint means roughly half the materials of a full BioDome. For someone with modest resources, this is the practical choice.

Constructability: The lean-to roof is architecturally simpler than reciprocal framing. Straight rafters sloping from the curved wall to the stone wall require less skill, fewer passes, and quicker installation.

Thermal Integration: The massive stone terrace wall acts as thermal mass and weather barrier. The clerestory window brings diffuse northern light without overheating.

Part Two: Planning & Preparation

Site assessment, materials sourcing, and design verification

Chapter 3: Site Assessment and Preparation

Evaluating Your Terrace Wall

Before committing to this design, thoroughly assess your existing wall structure. This determines the viability of the entire project.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: Wall Inspection Requirements
  1. Structural integrity: No large displacement, bulging, or recent collapses. Older is usually better‚Äîcenturies-old walls are proven.
  2. Height: Minimum 2.8m. The ridge of your roof will rest against or anchor into the top of this wall.
  3. Length: At least 10m of usable wall length where building won't interfere with adjacent structures or uses.
  4. Face condition: Reasonably flat or slightly curved (not severely convex). Small irregularities are acceptable.
  5. Base stability: The wall shouldn't show signs of tipping, settling unevenly, or undercutting at the base.
  6. Drainage: Confirm water moves away from the wall. You don't want your building to become a dam.

Site Clearance

Before building, remove all eucalyptus trees from the footprint and surrounding area. Use this protocol:

1

Clear Immediate Zone (0‚Äì15m from wall)

Remove all vegetation, dead wood, and debris. Level to create working space. This also reduces fire risk around the completed structure.

2

Harvest Eucalyptus for Building Poles

Select 2‚Äì7 year growth trees, 8‚Äì20cm diameter. Prioritize trees closest to your building footprint.

3

Treat All Cut Stumps Immediately

Within 15 minutes of cutting, paint stumps with 25‚Äì50% glyphosate or triclopyr solution. Mark treated stumps visibly. Check monthly for 18 months for any resprouting.

4

Source Remaining Materials Locally

Collect clay soil, sand, and granite from nearby. Use hand tools or animal power to minimize impact. Test clay quality (see materials section).

Testing and Sourcing Local Materials

Clay Assessment

Not all clay is suitable for cob. Perform this simple jar test:

1

Collect Clay Sample

Dig a hole 30cm deep. Collect soil from multiple locations on your property.

2

Fill Jar Test

Fill a mason jar 1/3 with clay soil, add water to fill, close lid, shake vigorously for 3 minutes.

3

Read Results After 24 Hours

Sand settles first (bottom), silt in middle, clay rises last (top). Good building clay has 15‚Äì30% clay by volume (top layer height). Below 15% = too sandy; above 30% = too much clay.

Sand Assessment

You need sharp building sand, not play sand. It should feel gritty and angular. Avoid rounded, smooth sand. Local excavators often have suitable material. Test by rubbing between your fingers‚Äîit should not form a smooth ball.

Granite Assessment

Ideal gabion stone is 10‚Äì25cm pieces with flat or slightly angular faces. Sorted stone creates a more stable structure than mixed sizes. Check local quarries for screening discards.

Part Three: Construction

Foundation, walls, and roof‚Äîstep by step

Chapter 5: Foundation (Semicircular Gabion)

The gabion foundation creates a raised, drained plinth that anchors the curved wall while maintaining the building-site separation from the terrace. It's half-circular, tying into the terrace wall at both ends.

Specifications

  • Shape: Semicircular arc (180¬∞), radius ~4.5m
  • Width: 0.6m (ring width from inner to outer face)
  • Height: 0.5m above finished grade, 0.3m below (total 0.8m depth)
  • Connections: Both ends tie into the terrace wall base using steel plates or through-stones

Excavation and Preparation

1

Mark the Arc

Drive a stake at the approximate center point (against the wall). Use a 4.5m rope and chalk to mark the semicircular line on the ground. Mark both inner and outer radii.

2

Excavate Ring Trench

Remove soil 0.3m deep between inner and outer arcs. Width should be 0.7m (slightly wider than finished gabion to allow room for stone filling). Level the trench bottom carefully‚Äîuneven excavation causes uneven settling.

3

Prepare Wall Connections

At both ends where the semicircle meets the terrace wall, trowel a layer of lime mortar into the wall base. This creates a bind point for anchor plates or through-stones.

Gabion Assembly and Filling

1

Position Gabion Cages

Set up galvanized wire mesh cages (3mm wire minimum, 10√ó10cm openings) around the semicircular trench. Position both inner and outer rings. Use stakes to hold in place temporarily.

2

Anchor at Wall Ends

Secure the ends of both inner and outer ring cages to the terrace wall using through-bolts or embedded steel plates set in lime mortar. This prevents the entire structure from spreading.

3

Fill with Stone (Bottom Half)

Fill the bottom 0.4m of the gabion with granite. Place larger, flatter stones on the exterior faces to create a finished appearance. Fill interior with smaller rubble. Compact every 15cm as you go.

4

Build Upper Walls of Cage

Once lower fill is stable (1‚Äì2 days), build the upper wall structure of the gabion cages to their full height (0.8m total above ground).

5

Fill Upper Half and Create Level Top

Complete filling to the top. The final 10cm uses flat, carefully selected stones to create a level bearing surface for the vertical wall poles.

6

Backfill and Grade

Once gabions are stable (allow 3‚Äì5 days), backfill the interior void area with soil to grade level. This creates the finished ground inside the ring.

Installation Timeline

The foundation can be completed in 2‚Äì3 weeks working part-time (weekends). Full-time, it's 5‚Äì7 days. Allow an additional week for initial settling before building wall poles.

Chapter 6: Wall Framework and Infill

The walls consist of vertical eucalyptus poles set into the gabion ring, horizontal poles woven between them, and cob infill. Only the curved front face needs walls‚Äîthe terrace wall serves as the back.

Pole Treatment (Critical for Longevity)

üî• All poles must be treated before installation

Untreated eucalyptus will fail in 5‚Äì10 years. Proper treatment extends pole life to 30‚Äì50+ years.

The five-step treatment process cannot be skipped:

1

Debark (Immediately After Cutting)

Remove bark within 48 hours of cutting. Use a drawknife or scraper. This prevents insect nesting under bark and allows preservative penetration.

2

Borax Soak (72+ Hours)

Mix borax and boric acid in a 12:8:80 ratio (borax:boric acid:water). Submerge poles completely. Weight them down‚Äîthey float. Duration: minimum 72 hours; longer (up to 2 weeks) is better. The borax penetrates the sapwood, creating a toxic barrier against insects and fungi.

3

Dry Under Cover (2‚Äì3 Weeks)

Stack treated poles on raised supports in a shaded, ventilated area. Protect from rain. Air circulation is essential‚Äîpoles will mold if in a closed space.

4

Char the Base (50cm Embedding Portion)

Before installation, lightly torch the lower 50cm of each pole to char the surface. This provides additional rot resistance where the pole meets soil/moisture. Don't burn deeply‚Äîjust darken the surface.

5

Seal with Hot Linseed Oil

Heat linseed oil to 60‚Äì70¬∞C (warm to touch but not steaming). Brush on all pole surfaces. The oil hardens over 2‚Äì3 days and waterproofs the wood. Reapply every 5‚Äì10 years.

Vertical Poles (Posts)

  • Quantity: ~14 posts on the curved semicircle + 2 posts against the terrace wall (one at each end)
  • Diameter: 15‚Äì20cm (untapered)
  • Total length: 3.3‚Äì3.5m
  • Embedded depth: 0.4m in gabion (ring diameter is 0.5m high)
  • Above-ground height: 2.5‚Äì3.0m (depending on position)
  • Spacing: ~1.2m apart (14 posts √ó ~9m arc √∑ œÄ = ~1.2m)
üí° Post Spacing Principle

Space posts evenly around the arc. Use a measuring tape and divide total arc length by number of posts to find spacing. Mark positions before starting installation.

Installation Steps for Vertical Poles

1

Prepare Pole Bases

Sharpen the bottom 50cm of each pole slightly (like a pencil point) or create a flat, broad base. This helps initial placement. For a more stable long-term connection, place poles on flat stones within the gabion rather than driving them.

2

Position and Plumb

Place each pole 0.4m deep in the gabion. Use a level to ensure vertical. Rock slightly to settle into stone. Check plumb in two perpendicular directions.

3

Brace Against Movement

Install temporary diagonal braces from poles to ground anchors. This prevents movement during subsequent construction. Remove braces once horizontal poles and cob provide lateral support.

Horizontal Wattle Poles

  • Quantity: ~7 horizontal levels √ó ~14 spans per level = ~98 pieces
  • Diameter: 5‚Äì8cm
  • Spacing: 25‚Äì30cm vertical gaps (approximately 7 levels over 2.5m height)
  • Attachment: Lashed with galvanized wire + natural fiber cordage to vertical poles

Installation Sequence

1

Mark Height Lines

Measure up each vertical pole and mark height positions at 25‚Äì30cm intervals. Snap chalk lines across multiple poles for alignment.

2

Lay Horizontal Poles

For each horizontal level, lay poles directly across the vertical posts, following the curved path of the semicircle. Poles between adjacent verticals should be straight (not curved to match the arc‚Äîthe curve is in the overall form).

3

Lash and Secure

At each intersection, wrap the horizontal pole tightly to the vertical post using galvanized wire (twisted tight with pliers) combined with natural lashing for flexibility. Use at least 2 wraps per intersection‚Äîone above, one below the horizontal pole.

Wall Infill (Cob)

Cob is a clay-sand-straw mixture that provides thermal mass, fire resistance, and sculptable form. It fills between the pole framework to create solid walls.

‚ö†Ô∏è Critical: The Drop Test

Form a ball of cob about the size of a grapefruit. Drop it from waist height (roughly 1m). Perfect cob should:

  • NOT shatter or break into pieces (too dry)
  • NOT flatten like pancake batter (too wet)
  • Slump slightly but maintain its shape

Adjust clay:sand ratio or add water accordingly. Get this right before building.

Cob Building Process

1

Prepare First Lift Base

Lightly dampen the gabion top surface to help adhesion. This prevents your cob from drying out and shrinking away from the foundation.

2

Build in Lifts (30‚Äì40cm per lift)

Apply cob in vertical sections, building up one 30‚Äì40cm layer at a time around the entire perimeter. Work around the whole arc in each lift‚Äîthis ensures even drying and prevents differential shrinkage.

3

Interlock Each Lift

Before adding the next lift, use your thumb to create deep holes in the top surface of the previous lift. These thumb holes provide mechanical grip for the next layer.

4

Embed Horizontal Poles as You Build

At each horizontal pole level, build cob around the pole, fully covering it. The poles become embedded in cob, creating a unified structure.

5

Allow Cure Between Lifts

Wait 1‚Äì2 days between lifts (depending on weather‚Äîfaster in dry conditions, slower in humid weather). Press your thumb into the previous lift; if it barely indents, you're ready for the next lift.

6

Final Wall Thickness

Build to a final thickness of 40‚Äì50cm (measured from outer pole face to inner surface). This provides adequate thermal mass and structural stability.

Cob Building Timeline

With ~8‚Äì10 cubic meters of cob needed, expect 4‚Äì6 weeks of part-time work (weekends). Full-time (with help), 2‚Äì3 weeks. The critical constraint is drying time between lifts, not labor time.

Chapter 7: Ring Beam and Lean-to Roof

The ring beam ties the wall tops together and provides the base for the roof. The lean-to roof is a simple structure: straight rafters rising from the ring beam to the terrace wall, topped with living plants.

Ring Beam (Half-Arc)

The ring beam is a continuous semicircular beam that sits on top of the cob wall, locking all wall posts and layers together. Both ends anchor into the terrace wall.

Specifications

  • Shape: Semicircular arc (180¬∞)
  • Radius: ~4.5m (sits on top of cob walls)
  • Diameter: 12‚Äì14cm
  • Construction: Two courses (inner and outer ring) or single larger beam
  • Connections: Both ends anchored to terrace wall with steel plates and threaded rod or pegged joints

Construction Method

1

Prepare Poles

Select 12‚Äì14cm diameter poles. These need the same treatment as wall poles. For a semicircular ring, you have two options: a single large pole bent to the arc, or multiple shorter pieces scarf-jointed together.

2

Create Scarf Joints (if using multiple pieces)

If using multiple pieces, create 50cm overlapping scarf joints between segments. Each joint is bound with wire lashing and pinned with wooden dowels. This creates a continuous, strong beam.

3

Position on Wall Top

Carefully place the completed ring beam on top of the finished cob walls. Ensure it sits stable and level (as level as the curved wall top allows). Check with a level at multiple points.

4

Anchor to Terrace Wall

At both ends where the ring beam meets the terrace wall, drill holes through the beam into the wall and set threaded rods (anchor bolts) with expansion anchors or epoxy. Alternatively, use steel plates and bolts. This prevents outward thrust.

5

Secure All Connections

Lash the ring beam tightly to the wall posts beneath with wire to ensure monolithic behavior during loads (wind, roof weight, seismic activity).

Lean-to Roof Structure

This is the simplest roof system we offer. Straight rafters rise from the ring beam to a ridge at the terrace wall, creating a single-slope roof. No central post, no reciprocal complexity‚Äîjust geometry.

Specifications

  • Rafters: ~14‚Äì15 pieces, 15‚Äì18cm diameter, treated eucalyptus
  • Spacing: Approximately 1.2m apart (matching wall post spacing)
  • Base: Rests on ring beam at curved wall (eaves height ~3.0m)
  • Ridge: Meets terrace wall at 5.0m height (nearly at or slightly above terrace wall top)
  • Slope: Approximately 25‚Äì30¬∞ pitch (1:1.8 to 1:2.4 rise:run)
  • Overhang: 1.0‚Äì1.5m beyond the curved wall for weather protection

Rafter Installation

1

Select and Treat Rafter Poles

Choose straight, knot-free poles 15‚Äì18cm diameter. Full treatment (debark, soak, dry, char, seal) is essential. Rafters will carry significant load and need longevity.

2

Calculate Rafter Angles and Lengths

For each rafter, measure the horizontal distance from ring beam position to wall, calculate the height rise (5.0m at wall, 3.0m at eaves). Use Pythagorean theorem to find rafter length. Create a template from the first rafter for consistency.

3

Prepare Rafter Ends

Cut a bird's mouth (notch) at the eaves end to seat on the ring beam. Cut a flat bearing surface at the wall end or create a mortise joint. Rafters can be pegged (using wooden dowels) or bolted for security.

4

Install Rafters Symmetrically

Install pairs of rafters (one on each side of center) simultaneously to balance the load. This prevents twisting or tipping during installation.

5

Brace Temporarily

Install temporary diagonal braces to prevent movement until all rafters are in place and the next structural layer (purlins) provides lateral bracing.

Roof Purlins (Secondary Structure)

Purlins are smaller poles laid perpendicular to the rafters, supporting the roof sheathing. In this design, 3‚Äì4 semicircular arcs of purlins provide sufficient support.

  • Quantity: 3‚Äì4 purlins, 8‚Äì10cm diameter
  • Arrangement: Semicircular arcs parallel to the wall, at varying heights
  • Spacing: Evenly distributed from eaves to ridge
  • Attachment: Lashed and pegged to rafters

Purlin Installation

1

Layout Purlin Positions

Divide the roof slope into 3‚Äì4 sections vertically. Mark positions on each rafter where purlins will sit.

2

Create Purlin Arcs

For each purlin height, create a continuous semicircular form using multiple pole segments, scarf-jointed together, or by selecting naturally curved pieces. Alternatively, use laminated (bent) construction.

3

Install and Secure

Position each purlin at its marked height, resting on all rafters. Lash and peg at each rafter intersection. Use at least two wraps of wire per connection.

Living Roof Assembly

The roof is finished with a living (planted) surface that provides fire protection, thermal mass, habitat, and beauty.

1

Roof Sheathing

Cover purlins and rafters with boards or rigid insulation. Options: rough-sawn planks (6cm thick), cork boards, or wood fiber boards (fast-draining to prevent water pooling). Fasten securely to purlins with wooden pegs or bolts.

2

Waterproofing Layer

Install EPDM pond liner (1mm minimum thickness). Overlap all seams by 30cm and seal with compatible sealant. This is the critical moisture barrier. Failure here leads to rot. Take time and do it right.

3

Root Barrier and Drainage

Lay a root barrier (landscape fabric or mesh) over the liner to prevent root penetration. Above that, place a drainage mat or layer of large gravel to ensure water moves downslope toward gutters.

4

Growing Medium

Spread 10‚Äì15cm of lightweight, fast-draining growing medium. A good mix: 40% pumice or perlite, 30% compost, 30% sharp sand. This provides nutrients and drainage while staying light (less roof load).

5

Planting and Establishment

Plant with drought-tolerant sedums, sempervivums, and native groundcovers. These require minimal water and maintenance. Space plants 15‚Äì20cm apart. Water lightly for the first month to establish roots, then largely drought-dependent.

Roof Timeline

Expect 3‚Äì4 weeks for rafter and purlin installation, sheathing, waterproofing, and planting (working part-time). Allow an additional 2‚Äì3 weeks for the living roof to establish before heavy rain season.

Chapter 8: Finishing and Details

Lime Render

The cob walls receive lime render for weather protection. Lime allows the wall to breathe, preventing moisture trap and maintaining the hygroscopic properties of cob.

Render Application

  • Base coat: 1:3 lime:sharp sand, 15mm thick. Apply with trowel after cob is fully dry (6‚Äì8 weeks minimum after final cob lift).
  • Finish coat: 1:2.5 lime:fine sand, 5mm thick. Apply 1‚Äì2 weeks after base coat is firm (not fully hardened).
  • Limewash: 3‚Äì4 coats of natural limewash applied over several months. This provides final weather protection and allows color adjustments.
üí° Lime Recipe Tip

Hydrated lime (calcium hydroxide) is easier to use than quicklime. When mixing, add water slowly while stirring. The consistency should be like thick yogurt for base coat, thinner for finish coat. Let mixed mortar sit 1‚Äì2 hours before use (slake time).

Windows and Doors

The Terraced BioDome includes:

  • Main entry door: 1.0m wide √ó 2.1m tall, centered on curved face (south-facing)
  • Side windows: Two windows on the curved face (east and west), 0.8m √ó 1.0m each
  • Clerestory window: Triangular window where roof meets wall, bringing diffuse northern light

Installation

Embed window and door frames in cob as you build walls. This is much easier than retrofitting. For wood frames, use chestnut posts pegged together with mortise and tenon joints (chestnut's dimensional stability prevents warping that would compromise weatherseals). Frames should be shimmed level during wall building and locked in place by cob infill.

Deep Window Reveals

Because cob walls are 40‚Äì50cm thick, windows create deep reveals (the thick wall recess on the interior). These are features, not problems. They provide:

  • Natural window seats (perfect for reading or observation)
  • Thermal buffering (the thick wall moderates temperature)
  • Controllable light and privacy (deep reveals reduce glare)

Entry Door Detail

The entry is the most exposed opening. Take extra care:

  • Set door threshold 15‚Äì20cm above finished grade to prevent water intrusion
  • Install a small covered porch or overhang to shelter the entry from wind-driven rain
  • Use a water-resistant timber for the threshold (split eucalyptus heartwood or oak)
  • Slope ground away from entry (minimum 2% grade)

Floor Finish

The floor sits on the leveled interior void created by the gabion ring. Options:

  • Earthen floor: Clay and sand mix with linseed oil finish. Beautiful, healthy, but requires regular maintenance. Ideal for dry climates.
  • Flagstone on sand: Local stone laid on 5cm sand bed. Durable, attractive, allows water drainage underneath.
  • Polished concrete: If you're using a concrete pad (heating, durability), polish or seal with natural wax for warmth and slip resistance.
üí° Earthen Floor Recipe

Mix 1 part clay, 2 parts sharp sand, 1/2 part straw (fine fiber), and tallow or linseed oil for binding. Spread 5‚Äì8cm thick, tamp down, and allow 2‚Äì3 weeks to dry. Seal with boiled linseed oil (multiple coats) for dust control and water resistance.

Part Four: Living Systems

Fire safety, water, and power for off-grid living

Chapter 9: Fire Safety and Defensible Space

üî• Fire Danger Protocol

This is a region where wildfires are possible. Your BioDome is built to resist fire, but the surrounding landscape must be managed aggressively. Complacency kills.

Structure Fire Resistance

The Terraced BioDome has exceptional fire resistance due to its construction materials:

  • Gabion stone foundation: Rock in wire mesh‚Äîcompletely non-combustible. Fire cannot damage or ignite stone.
  • Cob walls: Unfired clay-sand-straw mixture. Clay is essentially fired earth‚Äîit cannot burn. The straw content is encased in clay and will not ignite.
  • Dry stone terrace wall: The entire back wall is granite. This massive thermal mass absorbs heat without combustion.
  • Living roof: Growing plants hold moisture. The soil substrate and vegetation create a heat-absorbing, non-combustible layer.

The only combustible elements are the wooden door/window frames and the eucalyptus structural poles‚Äîbut these are fully enclosed within the non-combustible envelope of stone, cob, and living roof. Fire would need to breach these barriers before reaching any wood.

Native Forest Restoration: The Long-Term Fire Strategy

The most effective fire prevention is replacing eucalyptus with native forest. Eucalyptus is explosively flammable‚Äîvolatile oils in the leaves, shredding bark that carries fire up the trunk, and a tendency to drop burning branches. The catastrophic fires that have swept through Portugal and Galicia in recent decades burned almost exclusively through eucalyptus plantations.

Native Atlantic forests don't burn the same way. Oak, chestnut, and cork oak have high moisture content, don't produce volatile oils, and retain their leaves or bark in ways that resist ignition. A mature native forest is a firebreak, not a fire highway.

Your BioDome project is part of this restoration. Every eucalyptus you harvest for construction is one less fire ladder. Every native tree you plant in its place is long-term fire insurance. Within 10-15 years, the replanted zone becomes a living firebreak that protects not just your home but the entire landscape.

Defensible Space Zones

Zone Distance Treatment
Zone 1 0‚Äì5m Zero flammable vegetation. Mineral surface only (gravel, stone, bare earth). This is your immediate buffer against radiant heat and direct flame contact.
Zone 2 5‚Äì30m Mowed grass, no eucalyptus, maintained fire breaks. Young native trees (oak, chestnut, cork oak) are encouraged‚Äîthey will become your long-term protection as they mature.
Zone 3 30‚Äì100m Reduced fuel load, maintained access paths. Continue eucalyptus removal and native replanting. This is your transition zone to restored native forest.
üí° Replanting Priorities

Focus Zone 2 and Zone 3 replanting on fire-resistant natives: Quercus robur (pedunculate oak), Quercus suber (cork oak), Castanea sativa (sweet chestnut), and understory species like Arbutus unedo (strawberry tree). These species hold moisture, don't produce flammable oils, and create a closed canopy that shades out eucalyptus regrowth.

Gutter and Drainage

Install gutters around the roof perimeter. Guide water to filter and storage tanks. A first-flush diverter (simple, homemade) removes initial roof debris before water enters tanks.

  • Well (if ground water is accessible)
  • Regular trucked deliveries (for dry periods)

Chapter 10: Maintenance and Long-Term Care

Monthly Checks

  • Walk the structure perimeter. Look for new cracks, water marks, or damaged areas.
  • Check roof for debris accumulation or plant growth issues.
  • Inspect lime render for new cracks or spalling.
  • Test water system‚Äîcheck tanks, pump function, filter condition.
  • Verify solar system operation (if meter readings are normal).

Seasonal Tasks

Spring (March‚ÄìMay)

  • Full structural inspection. Look for winter damage, frost spalling of lime.
  • Repair any cracked render. Small cracks are normal and not dangerous, but seal large ones to prevent water intrusion.
  • Plant native species in restoration zones around the structure.
  • Service and test solar and water systems.
  • Clear gutters and drainage paths of winter debris.

Summer (June‚ÄìAugust)

  • Maximum fire vigilance. Monitor fire danger daily during dry periods.
  • Maintain defensible space weekly‚Äîmow grass, remove deadwood, watch for approaching vegetation growth.
  • Water living roof if extended drought (beyond 3+ weeks without rain).
  • Monitor stored water levels. If drawing down faster than expected, adjust usage or seek backup sources.
  • Check pole connections and lashing for any loosening from thermal expansion.

Autumn (September‚ÄìNovember)

  • Apply limewash touch-ups to any damaged or weathered render.
  • Clean gutters and water system before heavy rain season.
  • Inspect roof thoroughly. Check for any loose vegetation or debris.
  • Prepare for wet season: ensure drainage is clear, gutters flow freely.
  • Assess restoration progress. Note plant growth and native species recovery.

Winter (December‚ÄìFebruary)

  • Monitor for leaks during heavy rains. Interior ceiling inspection.
  • Check gutter drainage and ground-level water movement (prevent pooling near foundation).
  • Plan next year's improvements, repairs, or expansions.
  • Document ecosystem recovery. Photo comparisons over seasons are valuable.

Annual Maintenance

Pole Sealing: Every 5‚Äì10 years, reapply linseed oil to exposed poles. This keeps wood waterproof and maintains protection. Plan 1‚Äì2 days of work per year during dry season.

Living Roof Care: Sedums and native groundcovers require minimal intervention. In extreme droughts, water lightly. Remove any woody plant seedlings (trees or shrubs) that germinate‚Äîyou don't want a forest on your roof.

Lime Render Maintenance: Every 5‚Äì10 years (depending on climate), reapply limewash. This is more of a cosmetic task but provides meaningful weather protection. One or two coats is sufficient.

Monitoring Ecosystem Recovery

Your BioDome is part of a larger restoration project. Track this:

  • Eucalyptus eradication: Monitor stumps you treated. After 18 months, record success rate of different treatment methods.
  • Native species: Document trees and shrubs that appear naturally. Are cork oaks, wild olives, or other natives regenerating?
  • Water indicators: Spring flow returning? Soil moisture increasing? Well levels rising?
  • Biodiversity: Keep simple counts‚Äîbird species, insect diversity, mammal sightings. Even casual observation documents recovery.
